Behavioural Disturbance: Interactive Paramedic Student Trainer

Learn recognition, de-escalation, sedation decision-making, and post-sedation respiratory rescue for patients with severe agitation or mental disturbance.

🧠What is behavioural disturbance?

  • Acute agitation, aggression, confusion, or psychosis creating risk to patient, crew, or others.
  • May be due to mental illness, intoxication, hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, trauma, delirium, or mixed causes.
  • Always address reversible medical causes while managing safety.

🛡️First actions: safety + de-escalation

1) Scene: Maintain escape route, reduce crowd/noise, request early backup if risk escalates.
2) Communication: One lead speaker, calm tone, clear boundaries, offer simple choices.
3) Clinical screen: BGL, oxygenation, injury, tox, temperature, and mental state cues.
4) Escalation: Least restrictive strategy likely to succeed; document why.

⚠️ All sedated patients are high risk

All patients being sedated are high risk — seconds from disaster — all patients might die — sedation is a big deal (it is not innocuous).

⚠️Sedation medication safety warning

Understanding the pharmacological properties of the medications you are administering including their mechanism of action, indications, adverse effects, contraindications, precautions and warnings and interactions of key sedatives is essential for effective patient safety and management.

Furthermore, this comprehensive understanding allows you to better assess the benefits and risks with a holistic view of the patient’s condition, including potential differential diagnoses. This will enable you to explore alternatives or decide to withhold medication when the potential risks to the patient outweigh the benefits.

Before giving a medication: it is good practice to refer to your medication calculator and pharmacology reference to double check indications, contraindications, routes of administration, and dosage.

Precautions and warnings
Olanzapine may cause respiratory depression or worsen depression associated with alcohol or benzodiazepine use.
Olanzapine may cause orthostatic hypotension; use cautiously in people whose condition may worsen if this occurs or in those with risk factors for hypotension, e.g. hypovolaemia, taking an anti-hypertensive.
Patients with limited physiological reserves and the elderly have an increased risk of adverse effects.
Olanzapine should be used cautiously in patients who have a history of seizures or are subject to factors which may lower the seizure threshold.
Effects on ability to drive and operate machines: Patients must be advised that olanzapine may cause drowsiness and may increase the effects of alcohol, cannabis or sleeping tablets. If affected, they do not drive or operate machinery.
