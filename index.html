De-escalation Techniques

Summary: Lead with least restrictive communication and dynamic risk assessment.

  • One lead communicator, one safety observer, one clear egress plan.
  • Reduce triggers: lower tone, fewer people speaking, reduce lights/noise.
  • Offer realistic choices: location, seating, hydration, pacing options.

Treatment, Sedation & MOA

Summary: Identify reversible causes first, then escalate treatment proportionately when required for safety.

Droperidol

Use: Acute agitation/sedation in selected protocols.

MOA: Predominantly dopamine D2 receptor antagonism with sedative and antiemetic effects.

Midazolam

Use: Rapid anxiolysis/sedation when clinically indicated.

MOA: Benzodiazepine; enhances GABA-A activity, increasing inhibitory neurotransmission.

Olanzapine

Use: Antipsychotic option in some behavioural disturbance pathways.

MOA: Multi-receptor antagonism (including dopamine and serotonin pathways), supporting antipsychotic and calming effects.

Other options (service-dependent)

Consider local CPG-approved alternatives and patient factors (airway risk, intoxication, trauma, age, comorbidities).

Always follow the current edition of your service CPG and clinical governance instructions for indication, dose, route, and monitoring.

Legal Framework (NSW Mental Health Act)

Quick guide (prehospital focus):

  • Section 20 (Ambulance officers): Enables ambulance officers to detain and take a person for mental health assessment when statutory criteria are met (e.g., apparent mental illness/mental disorder with concern for safety).
  • Section 22 (Police): Police can apprehend and take a person to a declared mental health facility for assessment when criteria are met.
  • Section 81 (Transport): Covers transport to/from mental health facilities and supports practical transport arrangements, including use of authorised transport persons/services.
  • Also relevant: Section 19 (grounds for involuntary assessment/detention pathway) and Section 27 (medical examination after arrival) are commonly linked to prehospital handover decisions.

Operational tip: powers can shift with changing circumstances; if risk or presentation changes, reassess legal authority and document your reasoning and handover timeline.
