Behavioural Disturbance: Interactive Paramedic Student Trainer

Learn recognition, de-escalation, sedation decision-making, and post-sedation respiratory rescue for patients with severe agitation or mental disturbance.

🧠What is behavioural disturbance?

  • Acute agitation, aggression, confusion, or psychosis creating risk to patient, crew, or others.
  • May be due to mental illness, intoxication, hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, trauma, delirium, or mixed causes.
  • Always address reversible medical causes while managing safety.

Use current NSW Ambulance CPGs and local policy for exact thresholds, drugs, and dose pathways.

🛡️First actions: safety + de-escalation

1) Scene: Maintain escape route, reduce crowd/noise, request early backup if risk escalates.
2) Communication: One lead speaker, calm tone, clear boundaries, offer simple choices.
3) Clinical screen: BGL, oxygenation, injury, tox, temperature, and mental state cues.
4) Escalation: Least restrictive strategy likely to succeed; document why.

⚖️NSW Mental Health Act quick compare

Section 20: Power for ambulance clinicians to detain/transport a person for mental health assessment when criteria are met and safety is at risk.

Section 22: Police apprehension power for persons believed mentally ill/disordered and needing immediate care/control; often used when violence/security risk is higher.

Frisk search: Consider police support where lawful and necessary for weapons risk; paramedics should not exceed their legal authority. Use joint planning and clear documentation.

Students should verify exact wording/thresholds in the current Act and service directives.

💉Sedation + respiratory depression simulator

Patient still agitated. Continue verbal de-escalation and reassessment.
  • Increasing sedation can reduce agitation but increases respiratory risk.
  • Prepare airway tools, oxygen, capnography if available, and ventilation plan before sedation.
