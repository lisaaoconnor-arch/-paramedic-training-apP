The Roman Times

Dictator Caesar
Slain by Senators

By Ben Carew-Ferguson

Rome, March 15—

Julius Caesar, the Roman dictator, has been ambushed and stabbed 23 times at the Theatre of Pompey in Rome where the Senate was meeting. The conspirators were led by Marcus Junius Brutus and Gaius Cassius Longinus.

It is believed that Caesar was assassinated in a planned political attack. The senators stabbed him multiple times because they feared he was becoming too powerful and might end the Roman Republic.

On the “Ides of March” (March 15, 44 BCE), Caesar was at a meeting of the Roman Senate at the Theatre of Pompey in Rome. Around 60 senators had secretly planned to kill him.

The conspirators surrounded Caesar as if they wanted to present a request. One senator grabbed his toga, which was the signal to attack. The group then surrounded Caesar and then stabbed him many times with daggers as Caesar tried to shield himself from the attack.

According to witnesses Caesar was shocked to see his friend Marcus Junius Brutus among the attackers and was heard saying “Et tu, Brute?” (“You too, Brutus?”).

Caesar then succumbed to the violent attack and is said to have collapsed and died near a statue of his former rival, Pompey the Great.

Julius Caesar has been one of the most significant figures in Roman history because his leadership changed the course of the Roman Republic and helped lead to the rise of the Roman Empire. Through his military victories, especially in Gaul, Caesar expanded Rome’s territory and increased its power and wealth.

Caesar also introduced important political and social reforms, including changes to the calendar, debt relief, and support for the poor. However, his growing authority and appointment as dictator for life caused fear among many senators, who believed he threatened the Republic’s democratic system.

Crowds have gathered in disbelief as word spreads of Caesar’s death across the Forum. Some have been seen cheering and others have been seen weeping in fear of what may come next. The conspirators claim they acted to ‘restore the Republic’ in Rome — but Rome now awaits the chaos that may inevitably follow.
