Behavioural Disturbance: Interactive Paramedic Student Trainer

Learn recognition, de-escalation, sedation decision-making, and post-sedation respiratory rescue for patients with severe agitation or mental disturbance.

🧠What is behavioural disturbance?

  • Acute agitation, aggression, confusion, or psychosis creating risk to patient, crew, or others.
  • May be due to mental illness, intoxication, hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, trauma, delirium, or mixed causes.
  • Always address reversible medical causes while managing safety.

🛡️First actions: safety + de-escalation

1) Scene: Maintain escape route, reduce crowd/noise, request early backup if risk escalates.
2) Communication: One lead speaker, calm tone, clear boundaries, offer simple choices.
3) Clinical screen: BGL, oxygenation, injury, tox, temperature, and mental state cues.
4) Escalation: Least restrictive strategy likely to succeed; document why.

⚖️NSW Mental Health Act quick compare

Section 20 (quoted): “An ambulance officer ... may take the person to a declared mental health facility if the officer believes on reasonable grounds that the person appears to be mentally ill or mentally disturbed ...”

Section 22(1) (quoted): “A police officer ... may apprehend the person and take the person to a declared mental health facility if the officer believes on reasonable grounds that ...”

Search/frisk note (quoted law link): Section 22(2) says police may apprehend “without a warrant” and may exercise powers in section 81 during transport. The Mental Health Act does not set out a standalone “frisk search” power for paramedics.

Source: NSW Mental Health Act 2007 (current in-force version) and AustLII section pages. Always confirm current wording/policy before use.

⚠️ All sedated patients are high risk

All patients being sedated are high risk — seconds from disaster — all patients might die — sedation is a big deal (it is not innocuous).

⚠️Sedation medication safety warning

Understanding the pharmacological properties of the medications you are administering including their mechanism of action, indications, adverse effects, contraindications, precautions and warnings and interactions of key sedatives is essential for effective patient safety and management.

Furthermore, this comprehensive understanding allows you to better assess the benefits and risks with a holistic view of the patient’s condition, including potential differential diagnoses. This will enable you to explore alternatives or decide to withhold medication when the potential risks to the patient outweigh the benefits.

Before giving a medication: it is good practice to refer to your medication calculator and pharmacology reference to double check indications, contraindications, routes of administration, and dosage.

💊Medication reference tabs

Olanzapine

It has antagonist properties across serotonin (5HT2A/2C, 5HT3, 5HT6), dopamine (D1–D5), and cholinergic muscarinic receptors.

Behaviour disturbance. Ambulance CPG Behavioural disturbance (CPG BD1).

FrequencyEffects
Common (>1%)sedation, dizziness, orthostatic hypotension, hyperglycaemia, peripheral oedema.
Infrequent (0.1 – <1%)EPSE, elevation of liver aminotransferases.
Rare (<0.1%)rhabdomyolysis, VTE, hepatic failure, multi-organ hypersensitivity syndrome, QT prolongation.

Known hypersensitivity to olanzapine or excipients in olanzapine orally disintegrating tablets.

Respiratory depression risk (especially with alcohol/benzodiazepines), hypotension risk, seizure-threshold caution, and drowsiness/driving warnings. Document advice in Health Care Record.

Ketamine

Ketamine is a non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonist with analgesic and dissociative properties. It acts centrally in the brain and spinal cord by antagonising NMDA receptors in the dorsal horn from painful stimuli causing a trance-like state characterised by analgesia and amnesia with retention of protective airway reflexes, spontaneous respirations and cardiovascular stability.

Ketamine produces a dissociative anaesthetic state. The normal electrophysiology and functions of the thalamic-neocortical and limbic systems are interrupted, leading to catalepsy and altered sensation, perception, and awareness.

Behavioural disturbance and procedural sedation context per local CPG and protocol.

May include emergence reactions, hypertension, tachycardia, hypersalivation, vomiting, laryngospasm (rare).

Contraindications depend on protocol context; check current clinical guideline and patient-specific risk factors.

Droperidol

Dopamine D2 receptor antagonist with anti-agitation and antiemetic properties. Blocks postsynaptic dopamine signalling in mesolimbic pathways.

Behavioural disturbance and antiemetic indications per protocol.

Sedation, hypotension, dystonia/EPSE, QT prolongation risk.

Check QT risk, allergy history, and local contraindication list before use.

Midazolam

Benzodiazepine that enhances GABA-A receptor activity, increasing inhibitory neurotransmission to produce anxiolysis, sedation, anticonvulsant effects, and amnesia.

Agitation management, seizure control, anxiolysis/sedation as protocol permits.

Respiratory depression, hypotension, paradoxical agitation, oversedation.

Use caution with severe respiratory compromise and interacting CNS depressants.

Continuous airway and ventilation monitoring required after administration.

Fentanyl

Potent μ-opioid receptor agonist that reduces nociceptive transmission in the brain and spinal cord, providing rapid analgesia with dose-dependent respiratory depression risk.

Moderate to severe pain where opioid analgesia is clinically indicated.

Respiratory depression, chest wall rigidity (rare/high dose), nausea, hypotension, bradycardia.

Contraindications vary by protocol; check allergy/opioid sensitivity and clinical context.

Titrate carefully, reassess respiratory status continuously, and prepare ventilatory support.

💉Sedation + respiratory depression simulator

Patient still agitated. Continue verbal de-escalation and reassessment.

  • Prepare airway tools, oxygen, capnography if available, and ventilation plan before sedation.
