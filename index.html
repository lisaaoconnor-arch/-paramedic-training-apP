Sedation & MOA

Interactive sedation pharmacology reference (education only).

Olanzapine

MOA: Antagonist at serotonin, dopamine, and muscarinic receptors.
CPG indication: Behavioural disturbance (CPG BD1).
Key risks: Sedation, hypotension, respiratory depression risk with other CNS depressants.

Droperidol

MOA: Butyrophenone antipsychotic with dopamine antagonism and sedative effects.
CPG indications: Behavioural disturbance (CPG BD1), palliative context (CPG PC03 where applicable).
Key risks: QT prolongation risk, hypotension, enhanced CNS depression with sedatives/opiates.

Ketamine

MOA: Non-competitive NMDA antagonist with dissociative and analgesic effects.
Use context: Severe agitation/pain pathways per current CPG and authorisation level.
Key risks: Emergence effects, hypersalivation, hypertension/tachycardia, airway complications in high-risk patients.

Midazolam

MOA: Benzodiazepine enhancing GABA-A inhibition causing anxiolysis/sedation.
Use context: Sedation and seizure/agitation protocols per CPG.
Key risks: Respiratory depression, oversedation, hypotension (especially with co-administered sedatives/opioids).

Fentanyl

MOA: Potent μ-opioid agonist providing rapid analgesia.
Use context: Moderate to severe pain management per CPG.
Key risks: Respiratory depression, bradycardia, hypotension, and additive sedation effects.
For exact dose, route, contraindications, and scope use the current NSW Ambulance CPGs and local governance. Open NSW CPG guidelines.
NSW Mental Health Act:

Section 20: “(1) An ambulance officer who provides ambulance services in relation to a person may take the person to a declared mental health facility if the officer believes on reasonable grounds that the person appears to be mentally ill or mentally disturbed and that it would be beneficial to the person’s welfare to be dealt with in accordance with this Act.

(2) An ambulance officer may request police assistance if of the opinion that there are serious concerns relating to the safety of the person or other persons if the person is taken to a mental health facility without the assistance of a police officer.”

Section 22: “(1) A police officer who, in any place, finds a person who appears to be mentally ill or mentally disturbed may apprehend the person and take the person to a declared mental health facility if the officer believes on reasonable grounds that–

(a) the person is committing or has recently committed an offence or that the person has recently attempted to kill himself or herself or that it is probable that the person will attempt to kill himself or herself or any other person or attempt to cause serious physical harm to himself or herself or any other person, and

(b) it would be beneficial to the person’s welfare to be dealt with in accordance with this Act, rather than otherwise in accordance with law.

(2) A police officer may apprehend a person under this section without a warrant and may exercise any powers conferred by section 81 on a person who is authorised under that section to take a person to a mental health facility or another health facility.”

Wernikes
Neurology · Nutritional Deficiency
Wernicke's Encephalopathy
Thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency — acute neurological emergency
Cause & Risk Factors

Thiamine deficiency causing neuronal injury in diencephalic and brainstem structures.

Chronic alcoholism, malnutrition, prolonged vomiting, eating disorders, post bariatric surgery, IV glucose without B1.

Classic Triad
  • Confusion / encephalopathy
  • Ataxia
  • Nystagmus / ophthalmoplegia
Prehospital Treatment
  • Give IV/IM thiamine before glucose.
  • Check BGL and treat hypoglycaemia after thiamine where possible.
  • Airway support, vitals, ECG, urgent transport.
Outcomes & Progression

Early treatment: ocular signs improve first, confusion improves over days.

Untreated: Korsakoff syndrome, coma, death.

⚠ Key Rule: Always give thiamine before glucose in suspected deficiency states.
